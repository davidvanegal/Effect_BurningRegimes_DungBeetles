# Effect of burning regime on dung beetle assemblage structure and dung removal rates in tropical grazing environments

Abstract: 
Fire is critical for various agricultural practices, including shrub control, enhancement of forage availability and quality, and parasite control. Yet, fire can adversely affect insect diversity, which is crucial for optimal pasture functioning. This study assessed the effects of burning regimes, defined by their intensity, frequency, and magnitude, on dung beetle assemblages in grazing environments. While burning regime did not affect the abundance, species richness, diversity, and dung removal by dung beetles, it exerted a significant influence on the species composition of these insects. The dung beetle assemblage differed among grazing environments characterized by low and high burning regimes. Functionally, burning regime strongly affected tunneler, coprophagous and necrophagous beetles, while other functional groups showed no discernible response. Yet, burning regime did not affect dung removal by dung beetles. These results demonstrate that burning regimes in grazing environments can alter the composition and functional groups of dung beetles, even when overall diversity and dung removal remain stable. **Implications for Insect Conservation**. Changes in species composition and the proportions of functional groups in response to burning regimes could negatively impact essential functions through time, such as dung removal, and the ecosystem services they provide. Understanding how faunal communities respond to fires is important for advancing conservation planning in fire-affected areas, thus helping to maintain soil health and improve livestock production.

Keywords: cattle ranching, ecological functions, fire disturbance, grassland, Scarabaeinae, secondary dry forest.
